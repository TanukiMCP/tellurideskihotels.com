# Telluride Ski Resort: Complete Guide (2025)

Telluride Ski Resort spans 2,000 acres across 13,150 vertical feet of the San Juan Mountains, offering 127 named trails serviced by 18 lifts. What sets Telluride apart from Colorado's megares orts isn't size—it's the combination of challenging expert terrain, sparse crowds, stunning scenery, and authentic mountain town character at the base.

This complete guide covers everything you need to know about skiing and snowboarding at Telluride: terrain breakdown, lift system, best runs for every ability level, ticket options, seasonal timing, and insider tips for maximizing your mountain experience.

## Telluride Ski Resort At a Glance

**The Numbers:**
- **Total Acreage:** 2,000 skiable acres
- **Vertical Drop:** 4,425 feet (8th highest in North America)
- **Summit Elevation:** 13,150 feet (Gold Hill)
- **Base Elevation:** 8,725 feet
- **Lifts:** 18 total (4 high-speed quads, 1 gondola, 13 other lifts/carpets)
- **Trails:** 127 named runs
- **Terrain Parks:** 4 parks, 1 superpipe
- **Average Annual Snowfall:** 309 inches
- **Season:** Late November to early April

**Terrain Breakdown:**
- **Beginner (Green):** 23% - 300 acres
- **Intermediate (Blue):** 36% - 440 acres
- **Advanced/Expert (Black/Double Black):** 41% - 560 acres

**Unique Features:**
- Palmyra Peak hike-to terrain (13,320 ft)
- Revelation Bowl (300 acres of advanced terrain)
- Gold Hill Chutes (expert tree skiing)
- Free gondola connecting town to Mountain Village
- Uncrowded slopes even during peak periods

## Understanding Telluride's Mountain Layout

Telluride's terrain divides into distinct zones, each with unique characteristics:

### Front Side (Main Mountain)

**Aspect:** North-northeast facing  
**Elevation Range:** 8,725 - 11,890 feet  
**Characteristics:** Groomers, intermediate cruisers, some steep pitches

This is where most skiers spend their time. The terrain accessible from the main lift network offers excellent variety for intermediates and advanced skiers, with wide groomers, consistent pitch, and spectacular views into the box canyon.

**Key Areas:**
- **See Forever:** Intermediate cruisers at mid-mountain
- **Kant-Mak-M:** Wide run that changes character (groomed blue or mogul black)
- **The Plunge:** Expert steeps (up to 39° in sections)
- **Bushwacker:** Sustained advanced run through aspens

**Best For:** All ability levels, first-time visitors, cruising days

### Revelation Bowl

**Aspect:** Northeast facing  
**Elevation Range:** 11,150 - 12,260 feet  
**Characteristics:** Open bowl skiing, 300 acres of advanced terrain

Accessed via Revelation Chair (Lift 12), this bowl opened in 2007 and transformed Telluride from a primarily expert steeps resort to one with legitimate advanced bowl skiing. The terrain offers consistent pitch, excellent snow preservation, and fewer crowds than the front side.

**Runs:**
- **Revelation:** Main bowl access
- **Apex Bowl:** Steeper option
- **Lower Revelation:** Fun roll overs and terrain features

**Best For:** Advanced skiers, powder days, afternoon skiing (sun hits late)

### Palmyra Peak

**Elevation:** 13,320 feet  
**Access:** 20-30 minute hike from top of Lift 9  
**Characteristics:** Expert-only hike-to terrain, steep and exposed

Palmyra Peak represents Telluride's most extreme skiing. The summit sits at 13,320 feet (highest lift-accessed skiing in Colorado at 12,570 ft plus hike). Multiple chutes and bowls descend the north face, all rated double-black diamond.

**Routes:**
- **East Face:** Steepest (45°+), exposed
- **Main Chute:** Classic line
- **West Face:** Slightly mellower, still expert-only

**Requirements:**
- Avalanche beacon, shovel, probe required
- Sign waiver at patrol station
- Check conditions (closed in high avalanche danger)
- Expert skiing ability essential

**Best For:** Expert skiers seeking challenge, powder hunters, bragging rights

### Gold Hill

**Aspect:** Various (multiple chutes and zones)  
**Elevation Range:** 11,000 - 13,150 feet  
**Characteristics:** Expert chutes, tight trees, variable aspects

Gold Hill represents Telluride's most varied expert terrain. Dozens of chutes, glades, and steep faces offer endless exploration for advanced skiers. Less trafficked than Palmyra, these areas often hold untracked snow days after storms.

**Notable Runs:**
- **Spiral Stairs:** Tight, steep mogul run
- **Air Strip:** Wide-open steep face
- **Gold Hill Chutes:** Multiple expert options
- **Dynamo:** Sustained steeps through trees

**Best For:** Expert skiers, tree skiing lovers, adventurous intermediates (some zones)

### Meadows (Beginner Area)

**Aspect:** South-facing  
**Elevation Range:** 9,500 - 10,100 feet  
**Characteristics:** Isolated beginner terrain, gentle slopes

The Meadows area provides Telluride's best beginner terrain. Isolated from fast skier traffic and serviced by dedicated slow-speed lifts, this zone offers a safe learning environment with consistent pitch and wide-open space.

**Runs:**
- **Dipsy Doodle:** Longest green run
- **Meadows:** Main learning area
- **Sundance:** Gentle cruiser

**Best For:** First-time skiers, nervous beginners, ski school

**Limitation:** Only 23% of mountain is beginner terrain, concentrated in this one area

## Lift System Guide

### High-Speed Quads (The Workhorses)

**Lift 4 - Chair Four:**
- **Services:** Misty Maiden, Palmyra Ridge, access to expert terrain
- **Vertical:** 2,210 feet
- **Ride Time:** 8 minutes
- **Crowds:** Moderate, busier on powder days
- **Why It Matters:** Main artery to upper mountain

**Lift 5 - Gold Hill Express:**
- **Services:** Upper mountain terrain, expert runs
- **Vertical:** 1,652 feet
- **Ride Time:** 6 minutes
- **Crowds:** Light to moderate
- **Why It Matters:** Accesses Gold Hill expert terrain

**Lift 9 - Prospect Express:**
- **Services:** Beginner terrain, Meadows area
- **Vertical:** 549 feet
- **Ride Time:** 4 minutes
- **Crowds:** Light (mostly beginners)
- **Why It Matters:** Main beginner lift

**Lift 10 - Village Express:**
- **Services:** Mountain Village to mid-mountain
- **Vertical:** 1,546 feet
- **Ride Time:** 6 minutes
- **Crowds:** Heaviest in morning (base upload)
- **Why It Matters:** Main access from Mountain Village lodging

### The Free Gondola (Lift 1)

**Gondola - Telluride to Mountain Village:**
- **Route:** Historic Telluride to Mountain Village
- **Vertical:** 1,700 feet
- **Ride Time:** 13 minutes
- **Cost:** FREE (yes, really)
- **Hours:** 6:30 AM - midnight (winter)
- **Capacity:** Cabins seat 8

**Why It's Special:**
The only free gondola in North America connecting a town to a ski resort. This transforms Telluride from traditional ski area to integrated community where you can stay downtown and ski Mountain Village without driving.

**Strategic Use:**
- Morning: Downtown to Mountain Village (7-9 AM busy)
- Afternoon: Many ski down to town, ride up empty
- Evening: Access downtown restaurants from Mountain Village
- Night: Runs until midnight for après-ski

### Other Key Lifts

**Lift 12 - Revelation:**
- Accesses 300-acre Revelation Bowl
- Can be windy (closes in high winds)
- Worth the ride for advanced skiers

**Plunge Lift:**
- Serves steep expert terrain
- Short ride, quick laps possible
- Often light crowds (expert-only access)

**Beginner Lifts:**
- Several slow-speed chairs and magic carpets
- Concentrated at Meadows and base areas
- Appropriate pace for learners

## Snow Conditions & Best Times to Ski

### Season Timing

**Opening:** Typically Wednesday before Thanksgiving (late November)  
**Closing:** Early to mid-April (weather dependent)  
**Peak Season:** Christmas through March

### Monthly Breakdown

**November:**
- **Snow:** Limited, early season
- **Coverage:** 30-50% of terrain open
- **Crowds:** Very light
- **Rates:** Lowest of season
- **Worth It?** Only if you're flexible and accept limited terrain

**December:**
- **Snow:** Building base, variable
- **Coverage:** 60-80% by Christmas
- **Crowds:** Light early, packed Christmas week
- **Rates:** Moderate, spike over holidays
- **Worth It?** Yes, especially first two weeks (uncrowded, decent snow)

**January:**
- **Snow:** Prime! Coldest month, best powder
- **Coverage:** 100% of terrain
- **Crowds:** Light to moderate (avoid MLK weekend)
- **Rates:** Moderate
- **Worth It?** BEST MONTH - cold temps, deep snow, fewer crowds

**February:**
- **Snow:** Excellent, frequent storms
- **Coverage:** 100%
- **Crowds:** Moderate to heavy (Presidents Day week)
- **Rates:** Higher (peak season)
- **Worth It?** Yes, but book early and avoid Presidents Day if possible

**March:**
- **Snow:** Still good, warming temps
- **Coverage:** 100%
- **Crowds:** Moderate to heavy (spring break)
- **Rates:** High early, dropping late month
- **Worth It?** Yes - great snow, warmer temps, longer days

**April:**
- **Snow:** Spring conditions, corn snow
- **Coverage:** Declining, upper mountain closes mid-month
- **Crowds:** Light
- **Rates:** Low (best deals of season)
- **Worth It?** If you enjoy spring skiing and accept limited terrain

### Powder Days: Strategy

Telluride receives 309 inches of annual snowfall, though not as much as resorts north (Steamboat, Winter Park). When it does snow, here's how to maximize:

**1. Hit Revelation Bowl Early**
- Northeast facing holds powder longer
- Less traffic than main mountain
- Ride Revelation chair first

**2. Hike Palmyra**
- 20-30 minute effort = untracked lines
- Most people skip it (your advantage)
- Check conditions/avalanche danger first

**3. Explore Gold Hill**
- Multiple zones, less trafficked
- Trees hold powder days after storms
- Requires navigation skills

**4. Avoid:**
- Misty Maiden (packed out first by 10 AM)
- Lift 10 base (crowds flock here)
- Groomers (they'll be there tomorrow)

## Getting Around the Mountain

### First Run of the Day Strategy

**Staying Downtown:**
1. Take gondola up (6:30 AM first ride)
2. Ski down front side OR
3. Ride Lift 10 to access upper mountain
4. Hit favorite runs before crowds

**Staying Mountain Village:**
1. Ski-in/ski-out direct to slopes
2. Upload via Lift 10 (can be busy 8-9 AM)
3. Alternative: Hike up and access Lift 4
4. Beat gondola crowd to fresh groomers

### Lunch Strategy

**On-Mountain Options:**
- **Gorrono Ranch** (mid-mountain): Sit-down restaurant, bar, deck
- **Bon Vivant** (Market Square): Cafeteria style, fast
- **Giuseppe's** (Market Square): Pizza, pasta, quick
- **Taco del Gnar** (Mountain Village base): Fast tacos

**Money-Saving Tip:** Pack a lunch. On-mountain dining is expensive ($15-25/person). Many skiers brown-bag it.

**Best Spots:**
- Deck at Gorrono Ranch (sunny, views)
- Outside Lift 4 base (sunny, protected)
- Your hotel room (cheapest)

### End-of-Day Options

**Option 1: Ski to Downtown Telluride**
- Take Coonskin/Telluride Trail
- Long, mellow run (perfect for tired legs)
- Ski directly to lodging if downtown
- Can be crowded 3:30-4 PM

**Option 2: Gondola Down**
- Take gondola from Mountain Village
- Avoid ski-down crowds
- Good if exhausted or poor snow conditions

**Option 3: Stay Mountain Village**
- Ski to your hotel
- Shower, relax
- Gondola down for dinner later

## How Telluride Compares to Other Resorts

**vs Vail:**
- Telluride: Smaller, less crowded, steeper, authentic town
- Vail: Larger (5,289 acres), easier access, more dining, very crowded

**vs Aspen (Snowmass/Ajax/Buttermilk):**
- Telluride: Less expensive, less crowded, similar expert terrain
- Aspen: 4 mountains, more nightlife, celebrity scene, pricier

**vs Jackson Hole:**
- Telluride: Warmer, less extreme, better beginner terrain
- Jackson: More extreme, 4,139 vertical, colder, more snow

**vs Steamboat:**
- Telluride: Steeper terrain, less snow, less crowded
- Steamboat: More snow (349" annually), better trees, less steep

**Telluride's Unique Advantage:** The best lift-line-to-terrain-quality ratio in Colorado. Access expert runs with minimal wait.

## Insider Tips

**1. Avoid the Gondola Morning Rush**
Stay in Mountain Village if skiing is your #1 priority. The 8-9 AM gondola upload can add 20 minutes to your mountain access.

**2. Palmyra is Worth the Hike**
That 20-minute bootpack filters out 90% of skiers. You'll often have entire faces to yourself.

**3. Revelations Bowl Skis Better Than It Looks**
The chair ride makes it appear flat. It's not. 300 acres of excellent terrain that holds powder.

**4. Explore Gold Hill**
Most visitors stick to groomed runs. Gold Hill chutes offer expert terrain without the Palmyra hike.

**5. Ski Tuesday-Thursday**
Weekend crowds (such as they are) thin dramatically midweek. You'll lap runs without touching your poles.

## Final Thoughts

Telluride Ski Resort rewards skiers who value quality over quantity. While the 2,000 acres can't match Vail's 5,289, the experience of skiing challenging terrain without lift lines creates memories that crowded megasorts can't replicate.

The resort's 41% expert terrain makes this an advanced skier's paradise, though intermediates will find plenty of cruisers. Beginners should consider other resorts unless traveling with advanced skiers who don't mind separating.
